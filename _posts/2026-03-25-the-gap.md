title: "The Gap"
---

Something has shifted in the last year or two. The barrier to building something that looks and functions like a real product has dropped significantly. A founder with no engineering background can prompt their way to a working prototype. A designer can ship a functional tool without writing a line of code. A product manager can validate an idea without putting it on a roadmap. That's genuinely useful. Ideas that would have died waiting for engineering resources now have a path to exist.

But there's a gap between a working prototype and a product that works at scale, and that gap is where software engineers live.

The apps being built this way tend to share a pattern. Authentication that mostly works (if it has any at all). A database schema that made sense for the first fifty users. API calls made directly from the frontend. No error handling worth mentioning. No logging. No consideration for what happens when many people use it simultaneously, or what happens when the third party service it depends on goes down.

None of that matters for a prototype. All of it matters the moment someone decides to grow it.

What's interesting is that this doesn't reduce the need for engineers, but it changes when they step in. The first version of something can now exist without them. The second version almost certainly can't. And the second version is harder to build than it used to be, because now there's existing code that nobody fully understands, decisions that were made implicitly by a model rather than explicitly by a person, and a founder who is used to moving fast and is now being told that the foundation needs to be rebuilt before the next floor can go up.

The work that requires experienced engineers hasn't gone away. It's just further down the timeline, and there's often more of it by the time it arrives.

There's also a subtler shift happening. As more non-engineers build and ship software, the ability to translate between what something does and what it should do becomes more valuable, not less. An experienced engineer who can look at an AI-generated codebase, understand it quickly, identify what needs to change, and communicate that clearly to someone who doesn't think in systems. That's a different and arguably more demanding version of the job than it used to be.

The idea is the easy part now. Scaling it still isn't.
